For the first discussion prompt, I will use an example of a butterfly wing.

Many natural patterns emerge from chemical processes that occur while the organism displaying the patterns develops. Butterfly wings are made up of a variety of designs, and each species has its own unique pattern combination. The nymphalid groundplan is thought to be the basis for butterfly wing colour patterns. The butterfly serves as an excellent illustration of natural patterns. These gorgeous critters have a pair of distinct wings that mirror each other in design. Butterflies and moths are two examples of bilaterally symmetrical organisms. They have a single line of symmetry running down the centre of their bodies, which means their wing patterns are identical on both sides. The patterns on one side of a butterfly's wing are substantially different from the ones on the other side. Butterflies employ muted colours and patterns on one wing surface to conceal from predators and flashy colours on the other to attract mates. A great number of colourful scale cells cover the butterfly wings.
